Yes, Everything Is Pink.

Intentionally. Here's the evidence that it's actually a great idea.

🎨

Pink Is a Missing Value

Pink has no wavelength in the visible light spectrum — the brain imputes it by blending red and violet signals. As a data scientist, I deeply respect a colour that exists purely through intelligent gap-filling.

〰️

Pink Noise Is a Real Signal Type

In signal processing, pink noise (1/f noise) sits between white noise (pure random) and red noise (heavily correlated). It's the balanced middle ground — structured enough to be meaningful, varied enough to stay interesting.

📊

Pink = High Value in Heatmaps

In correlation matrices and heatmaps, deep pink and red flag the strongest positive relationships in the data — the most significant, the most worth investigating. I aspire to be a strong positive correlation on any team.

🔢

The Numbers: RGB(255, 105, 180)

Hot pink in RGB: R channel maxed at 255, G at 105, B at 180. The red channel is doing the heavy lifting while green and blue provide balance. Honestly, a solid team dynamic.

🔬

A Controlled Experiment With Results

Baker-Miller Pink was tested in a controlled study and measurably reduced aggression in participants within 15 minutes. Statistically significant. Peer-reviewed. This portfolio is evidence-based design.

🌡️

Colour Temperature: The Outlier

In HSL colour space, pink sits near hue 350° — just shy of completing a full rotation back to red. It's technically an outlier on the wheel. The best data points usually are.

TL;DR — pink is a missing value the brain intelligently imputes, a balanced signal type, and a high-correlation indicator. The colour choice was always data-driven.
